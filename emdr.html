What it is

What is EMDR?

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing) is a structured therapy that helps your brain reprocess difficult or traumatic memories. It uses bilateral stimulation, usually side-to-side eye movements, taps, or sounds, while you bring a memory to mind in a contained way.

You don't need to describe what happened in detail. EMDR works with the way the memory is held in your body and brain, not by retelling the story. For many people, this makes it more accessible than therapies that require extensive verbal recall.

EMDR is widely recognised and evidence-based for trauma and PTSD. It is recommended as a first-line treatment for PTSD and trauma by a range of leading bodies, including:

  • World Health Organization
  • American Psychological Association
  • Phoenix Australia
  • International Society for Traumatic Stress Studies
How it works

How EMDR works.

When something distressing happens, the memory can get stored in a way that keeps it feeling raw. The sights, sounds, body sensations, and beliefs about yourself stay tangled together, so that even years later, something small can trigger the whole package.

EMDR helps your brain do what it would do naturally during sleep: sort the memory, file it away, and take the charge out of it. The bilateral stimulation appears to support this reprocessing while you stay grounded in the present.

You stay aware and in control throughout. We move at your pace. If something becomes too intense, we pause and use grounding techniques before continuing.

Who it can help

Who EMDR can help.

EMDR is most well-established for trauma and PTSD, and is also well-suited for people experiencing anxiety, depression, or distressing memories where these are connected to past experiences or adverse life events.

Research suggests EMDR may achieve results in fewer sessions compared to some other approaches, and current evidence shows it is as effective as other leading trauma-focused therapies. It has also shown benefit for individuals with complex mental health disorders, a history of childhood abuse, and sexual assault survivors.

The technique is safe for adults and children, with protocols adapted to suit different developmental stages, and is flexible across a range of personal needs and backgrounds.

What sessions look like

What a course of EMDR looks like.

EMDR therapy follows a structured eight-phase model. Not every phase happens in every session, and some phases overlap or repeat. The pace is set by you and what feels manageable.

  1. History taking

    We talk about what brings you to therapy and identify the experiences that feel most significant. No detailed retelling required.

  2. Preparation

    We build skills for managing distress between sessions and during reprocessing. This includes grounding, resourcing, and discussing what to expect.

  3. Assessment

    We identify the specific memory, image, belief, emotion, and body sensation we'll work with for that session.

  4. Desensitisation

    This is the bilateral stimulation phase. The memory's emotional intensity reduces as your brain reprocesses it.

  5. Installation

    A more adaptive belief about yourself is strengthened in connection with the memory.

  6. Body scan

    We check in with how the memory feels in your body now, and address any remaining physical tension.

  7. Closure

    Each session ends with grounding to make sure you leave feeling settled, regardless of where we got to.

  8. Reevaluation

    The next session begins by checking how the previous reprocessing has settled and identifying what to work on next.

Telehealth

EMDR via telehealth.

EMDR can be delivered effectively via telehealth, using a secure video platform. Bilateral stimulation can be done in several ways online, including following on-screen visual cues, self-tapping, or audio-based methods. We'll discuss what works best for you in the preparation phase.

Telehealth EMDR has the same eight-phase structure and the same grounding and safety practices as in-person sessions. It also makes EMDR accessible to people in regional and remote areas who may not have a trained EMDR therapist locally.

If you're considering EMDR

What to know before booking.

EMDR isn't always the right starting point

For some people, particularly those with complex trauma, dissociation, or significant current life instability, we may spend more time in the preparation phase before reprocessing. Sometimes another approach is a better fit, or works better alongside EMDR. We'll figure that out together at intake.

It's not a quick fix

EMDR can be effective in fewer sessions than some other approaches, but the number of sessions depends on what you're working with. Single-incident trauma may resolve in a small number of sessions. Complex or developmental trauma usually takes longer.
