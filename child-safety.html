Statement of commitment

Amelia Slater Community Wellbeing is committed to the safety, wellbeing, and rights of every child and young person who engages with the practice. Children and young people have the right to feel safe, to be heard, and to be treated with dignity, regardless of their background, identity, or experience.

The practice has zero tolerance for child abuse and is committed to acting on any concern about a child's safety or wellbeing.

1. About this statement

Amelia Slater Community Wellbeing is a private social work practice based in Numurkah, Victoria, working with children, young people, adults, and families across Australia via telehealth and locally in person.

This statement sets out the practice's public commitment to child safety and explains how that commitment is reflected in day-to-day practice. It is intended for clients, families, referrers, and the wider community.

The practice supports the National Principles for Child Safe Organisations and complies with the Victorian Child Safe Standards CHECK as they apply to a sole practitioner social work practice.

CHECK — whether ASCW is captured by the Victorian Child Safe Standards as a "relevant entity" under the Child Wellbeing and Safety Act 2005 (Vic), and the practice's regulatory category under the Social Services Regulator.

2. Children and young people at increased risk

Some children and young people face additional barriers to safety because of who they are or the circumstances they live in. The practice recognises that the following groups can be at increased risk of harm and works actively to be a safe and accessible space for each of them:

  • Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children and young people
  • Children and young people with disability
  • Autistic and other neurodivergent children and young people
  • Children and young people from culturally and linguistically diverse (CALD) backgrounds
  • Children and young people who are unable to live at home, including those in out-of-home care
  • LGBTQIA+ children and young people

The practice recognises that sensory, social, and communication differences can make Autistic and neurodivergent children more vulnerable to harm and less likely to be heard when raising concerns. The practice's approach prioritises lowering those barriers and centring the voice of the young person.

3. How child safety is practised

Practitioner suitability

Amelia Slater holds a current Working with Children Check (Victoria) CHECK and a National Police Check CHECK. Both are renewed in accordance with their respective requirements.

As an Accredited Member of the Australian Association of Social Workers (AASW), the practitioner is bound by the AASW Code of Ethics, AASW Practice Standards, and ongoing professional development requirements.

Trauma-informed and neurodivergent-affirming practice

The practice's approach to working with children and young people is trauma-informed and neurodivergent-affirming. This means:

  • Sessions are paced to suit the child's nervous system and processing needs, not a fixed timetable
  • Communication is adapted to the child's preferences, including non-verbal options where helpful
  • Sensory needs are taken seriously and accommodated in both telehealth and in-person settings
  • The child's autonomy and consent are respected as far as developmentally appropriate
  • Masking is not encouraged or rewarded

Engaging children and young people in their own care

Children and young people are entitled to be informed about, and have a say in, their own care. The practice supports this by:

  • Explaining what therapy or assessment involves in language the child can understand
  • Discussing privacy and what stays in sessions, and the limits of that, in clear terms
  • Inviting feedback about whether sessions are working for them
  • Supporting the child's right to pause or end a session if needed

Engaging families and carers

Where appropriate and with the child's awareness, families and carers are engaged in supporting the child's progress. The practice maintains clear communication with parents, guardians, and other support people involved in the child's care, in line with the child's consent and the practitioner's professional obligations.

Online and telehealth safety

Telehealth sessions with children and young people are delivered via CHECK a secure video platform that complies with Australian privacy standards. Specific safeguards for telehealth sessions with minors include:

  • Clear arrangements about who else may be in the room or environment at each end
  • Discussion of the child's privacy needs at home before sessions begin
  • Sessions are not recorded
  • Information about online safety is shared with families during intake

4. Mandatory reporting and information sharing

Statutory child protection in Australia is administered by state and territory governments. As an Accredited Mental Health Social Worker CHECK, Amelia Slater is a mandatory reporter under the Children, Youth and Families Act 2005 (Vic). This means there is a legal obligation to report a reasonable belief that a child or young person is at risk of significant harm to the relevant child protection authority.

The practice may also share information under:

  • The Family Violence Information Sharing Scheme under the Family Violence Protection Act 2008 (Vic), where information sharing is necessary to assess or manage family violence risk
  • The Child Information Sharing Scheme under the Child Wellbeing and Safety Act 2005 (Vic) CHECK, where information sharing supports the wellbeing or safety of a child

Where information needs to be shared, the practitioner will, wherever it is safe and possible to do so, inform the child and family in advance. Further detail on confidentiality and information sharing is set out in the Privacy Policy.

CHECK — AMHSW status (separate from accredited member), Child Information Sharing Scheme prescribed information sharing entity status.

5. Raising a concern

If a child is in immediate danger, call 000. If a child needs to talk to someone urgently, Kids Helpline is available 24 hours a day on 1800 55 1800.

If you have a concern about a child's safety or wellbeing in connection with the practice, or about the conduct of the practitioner, you are welcome to raise it directly. The practice takes all concerns seriously.

Concerns can be raised by:

  • Email: admin@ameliaslater.com

Concerns can also be raised externally with:

  • eSafety Commissioner: for online safety concerns, esafety.gov.au

6. Relevant frameworks and legislation

This statement is informed by:

  • National Principles for Child Safe Organisations (endorsed by all Australian governments, February 2019)
  • Victorian Child Safe Standards (11 standards, in effect since 1 July 2022)
  • United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child

Relevant Australian legislation includes:

  • Privacy Act 1988 (Cth)
  • Online Safety Act 2021 (Cth)
  • Family Law Act 1975 (Cth)
  • Charter of Human Rights and Responsibilities Act 2006 (Vic)
  • Child Wellbeing and Safety Act 2005 (Vic)
  • Children, Youth and Families Act 2005 (Vic)
  • Family Violence Protection Act 2008 (Vic)
  • Privacy and Data Protection Act 2014 (Vic)
  • Health Records Act 2001 (Vic)
  • Crimes Act 1958 (Vic)

Statutory child protection in Australia is administered by state and territory governments. Each state and territory has its own legislation governing child protection and reportable conduct.
